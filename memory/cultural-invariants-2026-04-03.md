## 1. Research Review: Linguistic Universals & Sound Symbolism

### 1.1 Key Findings from Recent Literature

**A. Sound Symbolism as Cross-Cultural Universal**
- Sound symbolism occurs when word sounds convey meaning independently of arbitrary convention
- High-front vowels (/i/, as in 'bee') universally associated with smallness, brightness, quickness
- Low-back vowels (/a/, as in 'balm') universally associated with largeness, darkness, slowness
- These associations appear across unrelated language families, suggesting biological/neural basis

**B. Phonetic Feature Patterns (Cross-Domain Correspondences)**
Research by Lacey et al. (2024) identified domain-specific phonetic feature weightings:

| Domain | Key Phonetic Features |
|--------|----------------------|
| Shape (rounded-pointed) | Rounded vowels, voiced fricatives |
| Size (small-big) | High-front vs low-back vowels |
| Brightness (bright-dark) | High frequency vs low frequency sounds |
| Weight (light-heavy) | Voiceless vs voiced consonants |
| Valence (good-bad) | Smooth vs harsh articulation |

**C. Laryngeal Sounds (/h/ and /ʔ/) as Morphological Universals**
- Laryngeals occupy unique status in phonological hierarchy
- Present across all language families despite phonetic erosion in Indo-European
- Retain primary phonemic role in "classical" and indigenous languages
- Serve as root-level distinctions in feature geometry trees

**D. Visual Word Form Area (VWFA) Evidence**
- Brain region specialized for letter/form recognition
- Location reproducible across individuals and cultures (x≈-43, y≈-54, z≈-12)
- Sensitive to line junctions and geometric letter features
- Supports existence of neural substrates for geometric pattern recognition in language
