## 1. Linguistic Universals: Phonetic & Prosodic Patterns

### 1.1 Cross-Cultural Acoustic Regularities (Science Advances, 2024)

A landmark study involving 75 researchers from 46 countries analyzing 55+ languages revealed **universal acoustic signatures**:

**Music vs. Speech Invariants:**
- Songs consistently exhibit **slower rhythms** than speech across ALL cultures
- Pitches in song are **higher and more stable** than speech
- Musical rhythms are **more regular and predictable** than speech patterns
- These patterns facilitate **social synchronization and group bonding**

**Mechanism:** Slow, regular, predictable melodies enable large-group singing — an evolutionary adaptation for social cohesion.

### 1.2 Phonetic Universals & Tendencies

**Strong Cross-Linguistic Patterns:**
1. **Vowel Duration Patterns:** Vowels are shorter before voiceless obstruents than before voiced consonants (widespread but not absolute)
2. **Intrinsic F0 Effects:** Higher vowels have higher fundamental frequency than lower vowels
3. **Syllable Onset Preference:** Universal tendency for syllables to have onsets (though not obligatory in all languages)
4. **Post-vocalic Lengthening:** Long format transitions between vocoids and following vowels trigger vowel lengthening

**Markedness Hierarchies (Universal Learning Difficulty):**
- Clicks (Khoisan) < Ejectives < Implosives < Plosives (acquisition difficulty)
- Front vowels < Central vowels < Back vowels (perceptual salience)

### 1.3 Prosodic Universals

**Intonation Functions:**
- **Boundary marking:** Pitch expansion before boundaries (universal)
- **Focus marking:** Pitch prominence on focused elements (universal)
- **Question intonation:** Rising patterns for questions (widespread, not absolute)
- **Declarative intonation:** Falling patterns for statements (widespread, not absolute)

**Tone Language Patterns:**
- Contour tones (rising/falling) are more easily learned than level tones
- Native intonation prosody influences L2 tone acquisition more than word prosody
- Pitch is universally used for both lexical meaning (tones) and utterance meaning (intonation)
